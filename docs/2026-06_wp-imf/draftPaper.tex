\begin{abstract}
This paper presents a model used to analyze the long-term output effects of closing public spending efficiency gaps and of reallocating public spending toward more growth-friendly uses.
The framework integrates expenditure-side fiscal policy and human capital formation within an endogenous growth model.
It builds on three strands of the literature: public infrastructure investment, human capital development, and endogenous growth through technology creation and adoption.
The accumulation of public capital is adjusted for efficiency, so that only a fraction of each unit of spending is converted into productive capital.
The model is parameterized separately for a representative advanced economy and a representative emerging market and developing economy.
Simulations show that reallocating spending toward human capital and infrastructure investment, and closing efficiency gaps, deliver sizable long-run output gains, with effects materializing over different horizons across spending categories and country groups.
The implied cumulative public-investment multipliers over 25 years are about 1 for advanced economies and 2.5 for emerging market and developing economies.

\bigskip

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Public spending, Spending efficiency, Public investment, Human capital, Endogenous growth, Fiscal multipliers

\bigskip

\section{Introduction and motivation}
%========================================================================

% --- Intro structure follows Sahm (2021), "Writing Economics Research Papers"
%     (literature/ClaudiaSahm_WritingEcon.pdf): one paragraph per element,
%     contribution-led. See docs note .sped.md > "Writing style".
% [1] MOTIVATION --- why the question matters; set the stage with facts.
Public spending is a central lever of economic policy.
Yet the same headline level of spending can deliver very different economic returns.
With public debt elevated and fiscal space limited in many economies, the quality of spending matters as much as its quantity.
Governments increasingly need their budgets to do more with less.
Two features of public budgets shape their long-run payoff.
The first is \emph{allocation}: how spending is split between current consumption and growth-enhancing investment in infrastructure, human capital, and innovation.
The second is \emph{efficiency}: how much of each dollar spent is actually converted into productive capital, as opposed to being lost to weak project selection, governance gaps, and implementation slippage.

% [2] RESEARCH QUESTION + CONTRIBUTION --- lead with our own contribution, then
%     set it against others in one sentence (substitutes for a lit-review section).
This paper develops and documents a model to answer a specific question: how much can a country raise long-run output by reallocating public spending toward more productive uses and by closing spending-efficiency gaps?
Its contribution is to integrate the entire expenditure side of fiscal policy---infrastructure, human capital, and R\&D---together with household human capital formation within a single endogenous-growth model in which both the allocation and the efficiency of spending are operative.
Where existing work typically isolates one channel---public-investment multipliers, human-capital formation, or innovation---this integration lets the model speak to the horizon over which each type of spending pays off and to how country characteristics shape the gains.

% [3] METHODS --- key features of the approach; the former strand-by-strand lit
%     review folded in here as context (citations kept), not as a separate section.
The model embeds these channels in an otherwise standard New Keynesian framework with endogenous growth.
Public investment builds productive infrastructure capital---roads, communication networks---that raises private-sector productivity, in the tradition of the public-investment and output-multiplier literature \citep{TraumYang2015, DrautzburgUhlig2015, IMF2020FM}.
Households accumulate human capital by devoting time to education and health, complemented by a stock of public human capital such as schools and hospitals \citep{BlankenauSimpson2004, Dissouetal2016, Atoliaetal2021}.
Growth is endogenous: following \citet{CominGertler2006} and \citet{Anzoateguietal2019}, the model separates the stock of created technologies $Z_t$, available for adoption, from the stock of adopted technologies $A_t$ in use, so that public R\&D raises $Z_t$ while costly private adoption expands $A_t$ and the gap $Z_t-A_t$ generates realistic diffusion lags.
Across all three channels, public spending is efficiency-adjusted \citep{Bergetal2019}: only a fraction of each unit spent is converted into productive capital.
The model is calibrated separately for a representative advanced economy and a representative emerging market and developing economy.

% [4] RESULTS --- the payoff; at most three findings, stated with numbers. Make them shine.
The main findings are threefold.
First, reallocating spending toward investment raises output over the long run, with infrastructure paying off sooner and human capital later.
Second, closing efficiency gaps delivers sizable additional gains, the more so for economies that start further from the efficiency frontier.
Third, the gains are larger for emerging market and developing economies, reflecting a higher marginal product of public capital.
The implied cumulative public-investment multipliers over 25 years are about 1 for advanced economies and 2.5 for emerging market and developing economies, with a multiplier of about 1.8 for R\&D.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:model} sets out the model.
Section~\ref{sec:calibration} describes the calibration for the representative advanced economy and emerging market and developing economy.
Section~\ref{sec:properties} examines the model's properties, including the implied fiscal multipliers and their sensitivity to key parameters.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents a set of policy experiments.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusions} concludes.
Appendix~\ref{app:equilibrium} collects the full set of equilibrium conditions.

\section{The model} \label{sec:model}
%========================================================================

The model is built on a relatively standard New Keynesian framework augmented with endogenous growth. 
Its central extension, for the purpose of analyzing public spending, is the introduction of productive public spending components: investment in physical infrastructure, investment in human capital, and spending on research and development. 
These outlays build, respectively, a stock of public infrastructure capital (e.g., roads and bridges), a stock of public human capital (e.g., schools and hospitals), and the economy's stock of technological knowledge (e.g., publicly funded patents not yet adopted by the private sector).
All are accumulated from efficiency-adjusted public spending, recognizing that a fraction of each flow is wasted through inefficiency.

The economy is populated by a representative household, a multi-tiered production sector, and a government.
The household chooses consumption, labor supply, private investment, holdings of government debt, and---departing from the standard framework---time devoted to human capital accumulation.
The production sector has three tiers: final-good firms that set prices à la Calvo, intermediate-good firms operating Cobb--Douglas technologies, and technology adopters that convert created but unadopted technologies into usable ones.
The government comprises a fiscal authority and a central bank.
The fiscal authority spends on public infrastructure, public human capital, and public R\&D, in addition to government consumption and lump-sum transfers, and levies taxes on consumption and labor income.
The central bank sets the policy rate according to a Taylor rule.

The economy grows along a balanced-growth path driven by technology; the trend level is denoted $\Lambda_t$ and grows at the constant gross rate $g$.
The remainder of this section sets out the model's building blocks in turn, starting from households. The complete set of stationarized equilibrium conditions is collected in Appendix~\ref{app:equilibrium}.

\subsection{Households}

The representative household maximizes expected lifetime utility,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:utility}
    \mathbb{E}_0 \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t \left[\ln C_t - \omega \frac{\left(L_t + E_t\right)^{1+\varphi}}{1+\varphi}\right],
\end{equation}
where $C_t$ is consumption, $\beta \in (0,1)$ is the discount factor, $\varphi$ is the inverse Frisch elasticity, and $\omega$ scales the disutility of time spent away from leisure.
The household's unit time endowment is split between labor supply $L_t$, time devoted to human capital accumulation $E_t$, and leisure $1 - L_t - E_t$.
The flow budget constraint, in real terms, is
\begin{equation} \label{eq:hhbudget}
    \left(1+\tau_t^{c}\right) C_t + I_t + D_t = \left(1-\tau_t^{w}\right) w_t N_t + r_t^{k} K_{t-1} + \frac{R_{t-1}}{\Pi_t} D_{t-1} + T_t + \Omega_t,
\end{equation}
where $\tau_t^{c}$ and $\tau_t^{w}$ are the consumption and labor income tax rates, $I_t$ is private investment, $D_t$ is real holdings of government debt paying the gross return $R_{t-1}$, $\Pi_t$ is gross inflation, $w_t$ is the real wage, $N_t \equiv H_t L_t$ is effective labor, $r_t^{k}$ is the rental rate of private capital $K_t$, $T_t$ are lump-sum transfers, and $\Omega_t$ are profits rebated by firms and technology adopters.

Let $\xi_t$ denote the marginal utility of consumption. Optimal choices over consumption, debt, and private capital imply
\begin{align}
    \xi_t &= \frac{1}{\left(1+\tau_t^{c}\right) C_t}, \label{eq:mu}\\
    \xi_t &= \beta\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\xi_{t+1}\frac{R_t}{\Pi_{t+1}}\right], \label{eq:euler}\\
    \xi_t &= \beta\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\xi_{t+1}\left(r_{t+1}^{k} + 1 - \delta\right)\right], \qquad K_t = (1-\delta) K_{t-1} + I_t, \label{eq:capital}
\end{align}
where $\delta$ is the depreciation rate of private capital.

The novel block concerns human capital. Private human capital $H_t$ evolves according to
\begin{equation} \label{eq:humancapital}
    H_{t+1} = \left(1 - \delta^{h}\right) H_t + \chi \left(E_t\right)^{\gamma} \left(\frac{K_t^{GE}}{\Lambda_t}\right)^{\mu},
\end{equation}
where $\delta^{h}$ is the depreciation rate of human capital, $\chi$ is a scale parameter, $K_t^{GE}$ is the stock of public human capital (e.g., schools and hospitals), and $\Lambda_t$ scales public human capital to keep the relationship consistent with balanced growth.
The elasticities $\gamma$ and $\mu$ govern the response of human capital formation to the household's time input and to the public human capital stock, respectively.
Letting $\xi_t^{H}$ be the shadow value of human capital, the choices of labor and education time satisfy
\begin{align}
    \omega \left(L_t + E_t\right)^{\varphi} &= \xi_t \left(1-\tau_t^{w}\right) w_t H_t, \label{eq:labor}\\
    \omega \left(L_t + E_t\right)^{\varphi} &= \xi_t^{H}\, \chi \gamma \left(E_t\right)^{\gamma-1} \left(\frac{K_t^{GE}}{\Lambda_t}\right)^{\mu}, \label{eq:edutime}\\
    \xi_t^{H} &= \beta\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\xi_{t+1}\left(1-\tau_{t+1}^{w}\right) w_{t+1} L_{t+1} + \xi_{t+1}^{H}\left(1-\delta^{h}\right)\right]. \label{eq:hcvalue}
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{eq:labor} is the standard labor-supply condition; equation~\eqref{eq:edutime} equates the marginal disutility of time to the value of the human capital it builds; and equation~\eqref{eq:hcvalue} prices human capital by its discounted contribution to future labor income.

\subsection{Production and technology}

\paragraph{Final goods.} A competitive final good $Y_t^{d}$ is a CES aggregate of a continuum of intermediate varieties with elasticity of substitution $\epsilon$. Cost minimization yields the demand for variety $i$, $y_t(i) = \left(P_t(i)/P_t\right)^{-\epsilon} Y_t^{d}$, and the price index $P_t = \left[\int_0^1 P_t(i)^{1-\epsilon}\, di\right]^{1/(1-\epsilon)}$.

\paragraph{Intermediate goods.} A continuum of monopolistically competitive firms produces the varieties. Output of a firm is given by the aggregate production function
\begin{equation} \label{eq:production}
    Y_t = A_t^{\vartheta-1} K_{t-1}^{\alpha} N_t^{1-\alpha} \left(K_{t-1}^{GI}\right)^{\alpha_G} - \Theta,
\end{equation}
where $A_t$ is the stock of adopted technologies, $K_{t-1}$ is private capital, $N_t = H_t L_t$ is effective labor, $K_{t-1}^{GI}$ is the stock of public infrastructure capital, which augments private production as a positive externality, and $\Theta$ is a fixed cost.
The parameter $\vartheta$ governs the productivity impact of technology adoption, while $\alpha$ and $\alpha_G$ denote the output elasticities of private capital and public infrastructure, respectively.\footnote{In \citet{Anzoateguietal2019} a single intermediate-goods elasticity of substitution sets both the markup and the returns to variety. Here the markup is governed separately by $\epsilon$; $\vartheta$ retains only the latter role, mapping the stock of adopted technologies into TFP through the love-of-variety term $A_t^{\vartheta-1}$.}
Cost minimization implies a common real marginal cost $mc_t$ and the factor-demand conditions
\begin{equation} \label{eq:factordemand}
    r_t^{k} = \alpha\, mc_t \frac{Y_t}{K_{t-1}}, \qquad w_t = (1-\alpha)\, mc_t \frac{Y_t}{N_t}, \qquad \frac{K_{t-1}}{N_t} = \frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\frac{w_t}{r_t^{k}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Price setting.} Each period a fraction $1-\theta_p$ of firms reoptimizes, while the remainder index their price to past inflation with elasticity $\chi_p$. The reset price $\Pi_t^{*}$ solves the recursive system
\begin{align}
    x_{1,t} &= \xi_t\, mc_t\, Y_t^{d} + \beta\theta_p\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\left(\frac{\Pi_t^{\chi_p}}{\Pi_{t+1}}\right)^{-\epsilon} x_{1,t+1}\right], \label{eq:g1}\\
    x_{2,t} &= \xi_t\, \Pi_t^{*}\, Y_t^{d} + \beta\theta_p\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\left(\frac{\Pi_t^{\chi_p}}{\Pi_{t+1}}\right)^{1-\epsilon} \frac{\Pi_t^{*}}{\Pi_{t+1}^{*}} x_{2,t+1}\right], \qquad \epsilon\, x_{1,t} = (\epsilon-1)\, x_{2,t}, \label{eq:g2}
\end{align}
together with the aggregate price law of motion $1 = \theta_p\left(\Pi_{t-1}^{\chi_p}/\Pi_t\right)^{1-\epsilon} + (1-\theta_p)\left(\Pi_t^{*}\right)^{1-\epsilon}$.

\paragraph{Technology creation and adoption.}
Following \citet{CominGertler2006} and \citet{Anzoateguietal2019}, the model distinguishes between the stock of created technologies $Z_t$, available for adoption, and the stock of adopted technologies $A_t$ that enters production. The gap $Z_t - A_t$ is the stock of unadopted technologies.

The creation of new technologies depends on two factors.
First, higher human capital fosters innovation, reflecting a learning-by-doing channel as in \citet{ChangGomesSchorfheide2002}.
Second, public R\&D spending $G_t^{RD}$ contributes directly to creation:
\begin{equation} \label{eq:Z}
    \log\!\left(\frac{Z_t}{Z^{SS}}\right) = \rho_A \log\!\left(\frac{Z_{t-1}}{Z^{SS}}\right) + \left(1 - \rho_A\right)\left(\alpha_{HA} \log\!\left(\frac{H_{t-1}}{H^{SS}}\right) + \alpha_{RD} \log\!\left(\frac{\left(1-e_t^{GRD}\right) G_t^{RD}}{\left(1-e^{GRD}\right) G^{RD,SS}}\right)\right),
\end{equation}
where only the productive fraction $\left(1-e_t^{GRD}\right)$ of R\&D spending drives innovation: $e_t^{GRD}$ is the R\&D inefficiency wedge, equal to its steady-state value $e^{GRD}$ in the baseline and reduced when the R\&D efficiency gap is closed (Section~\ref{sec:results}). The raw spending $G_t^{RD}$ enters the government budget constraint~\eqref{eq:govbudget}.
To capture the lag between creation and adoption, the stock of adopted technologies evolves as
\begin{equation} \label{eq:A}
    A_{t+1} = \lambda_t\, \phi \left[Z_t - A_t\right] + \phi A_t,
\end{equation}
where $\phi$ is the survival rate of technologies and the time-varying probability of adoption is $\lambda_t = \lambda_0 \left(S_t/\Lambda_t\right)^{\varsigma}$, increasing in adopters' real expenditure $S_t$ scaled by the trend $\Lambda_t$.
Competitive adopters choose $S_t$ to maximize the value of converting unadopted technologies into usable ones, equating the marginal cost of adoption effort to its discounted marginal value.
The associated value functions and the optimality condition for $S_t$, together with the stochastic discount factor $\mathit{SDF}_{t+1} = \beta\, \xi_{t+1}/\xi_t$ used to value these flows, are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:equilibrium}.
The productivity payoff of the adopted-technology stock $A_t$ is realized in the production function~\eqref{eq:production}, with strength governed by $\vartheta$.

\subsection{Government: fiscal and monetary policy}

\paragraph{Public capital and efficiency.} The fiscal authority builds public infrastructure capital $K_t^{GI}$ and public human capital $K_t^{GE}$ through investment flows $I_t^{GI}$ and $I_t^{GE}$, adjusted for technical inefficiencies $e^{GI}$ and $e^{GE}$:
\begin{align}
    K_t^{GI} &= \left(1 - \delta^{GI}\right) K_{t-1}^{GI} + \left(1 - e^{GI}\right) I_t^{GI}, \label{eq:kgi} \\
    K_t^{GE} &= \left(1 - \delta^{GE}\right) K_{t-1}^{GE} + \left(1 - e^{GE}\right) I_t^{GE}. \label{eq:kge}
\end{align}
Only a fraction $1 - e^{x}$ of each unit spent is converted into productive capital. An analogous wedge $e^{GRD}$ applies to public R\&D, so that effective R\&D in equation~\eqref{eq:Z} is $\left(1 - e^{GRD}\right) G_t^{RD}$.\footnote{A second dimension of efficiency relates to allocating resources toward higher-return projects, referred to as allocative efficiency. Section~\ref{sec:results} also considers an extended scenario that incorporates both technical and allocative efficiency improvements.}

\paragraph{Budget and instruments.} Let $D_t$ denote real public debt and $d_t = D_t/Y_t$ the debt-to-GDP ratio. The government budget constraint is
\begin{equation} \label{eq:govbudget}
    D_t = \frac{R_{t-1}}{\Pi_t} D_{t-1} + G_t^{C} + I_t^{GI} + I_t^{GE} + G_t^{RD} + T_t - \tau_t^{w} w_t N_t - \tau_t^{c} C_t,
\end{equation}
where $G_t^{C}$ is government consumption. The four expenditure instruments---$G_t^{C}$, $I_t^{GI}$, $I_t^{GE}$, and $G_t^{RD}$---can be moved independently. In the policy experiments of Section~\ref{sec:results}, an increase in one growth-enhancing category is financed by an equal cut in government consumption, keeping the reform budget-neutral on impact. The tax rates and transfers adjust gradually to stabilize debt, responding to the deviation of the debt-to-GDP ratio from its target $d^{*}$.

\paragraph{Monetary policy and sovereign borrowing cost.} The central bank sets the policy rate $R_t^{mp}$ according to a Taylor rule,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:taylor}
    \frac{R_t^{mp}}{R^{mp}} = \left(\frac{R_{t-1}^{mp}}{R^{mp}}\right)^{\rho_R}\left[\left(\frac{\Pi_t}{\Pi}\right)^{\gamma_\pi}\left(\frac{Y_t^{d}}{Y^{d}}\right)^{\gamma_y}\right]^{1-\rho_R} e^{\varepsilon_t^{mp}}.
\end{equation}
The government borrowing rate $R_t$ tracks the policy rate plus a sovereign spread that rises with debt,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:spread}
    \log R_t = \rho_{RG} R_{t-1} + \left(1-\rho_{RG}\right)\left[\log R_t^{mp} + \Delta_t\left(d_{t-1} - d^{*}\right)\right] + \varepsilon_t^{spr},
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_t$ is the product of a debt-dependent default probability and the associated loss, with the default probability a logistic function of the debt ratio.

\subsection{Market clearing and equilibrium}

Goods-market clearing equates aggregate demand to its uses,
\begin{equation} \label{eq:resource}
    Y_t^{d} = C_t + I_t + I_t^{GI} + G_t^{C} + I_t^{GE} + G_t^{RD} + \mathcal{R}_t,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{R}_t$ collects the resources devoted to technology creation and adoption. Gross output exceeds value added by the price-dispersion wedge $v_t^{p}$ generated by staggered pricing, $Y_t = v_t^{p} Y_t^{d}$. A competitive equilibrium is a sequence of quantities and prices such that households and firms optimize, the government budget constraint and policy rules hold, and the goods, labor, capital, and bond markets clear, given the paths of the policy instruments and exogenous processes.

\subsection{Balanced growth and solution}

The economy follows a balanced-growth path along which real quantities grow at the constant gross trend rate $g$, driven by the trend $\Lambda_t$ in technology. All trending variables are stationarized by $\Lambda_t$ prior to solution; this is the role of the scaling terms in equations~\eqref{eq:humancapital} and~\eqref{eq:A}. The model is implemented in Dynare and solved as a deterministic, perfect-foresight system. The policy experiments in Section~\ref{sec:results} are permanent reforms introduced in 2025, with responses traced out to 2050 around the balanced-growth steady state.

\section{Calibration} \label{sec:calibration}
%========================================================================

The model is calibrated for a representative advanced economy (AE) and a representative emerging market and developing economy (EMDE), separately, at a quarterly frequency.
Conventional parameters governing preferences, pricing, and policy take standard values from the literature.
The parameters specific to the public-spending and growth channels, which are central to the experiments, are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:parameters} and described below; the steady-state fiscal ratios that follow are set to match observed spending shares and effective tax rates for each country group.

\begin{compactitem}
    \item \emph{Elasticity of output with respect to infrastructure ($\alpha_G$).} It is set at 0.05 for AEs, following \citet{TraumYang2015}, and 0.17 for EMDEs, based on \citet{BornLigthart2014} and \citet{Buffieetal2012}. The higher value for EMDEs reflects the larger contribution of public infrastructure investment to output in these economies.
    \item \emph{Private human capital accumulation.} The elasticity of human capital accumulation with respect to the public human capital stock ($\mu$) is set at 0.25 for EMDEs and 0.1 for AEs, which is in the range documented in \citet{GlommRavikumar1998}, \citet{Blankenau2005}, and \citet{Atoliaetal2021}. The lower value for AEs reflects the empirical finding that the marginal impact of government investment on human capital formation is moderate, given already well-developed education and health infrastructure. The elasticity with respect to the time spent on human capital accumulation is set to $\gamma = 0.5$ for both country groups, consistent with \citet{Atoliaetal2021} and \citet{Dissouetal2016}.
    \item \emph{Depreciation of public capital ($\delta^{GI}$, $\delta^{GE}$).} Both depreciation rates are set at the standard value of 0.025, corresponding to an annual depreciation rate of 10 percent.
    \item \emph{Technology innovation parameters.} The AR(1) coefficient for the stock of technologies ($\rho_A$) is set at 0.79 (quarterly), as in \citet{ChangGomesSchorfheide2002}. For AEs, the elasticity of human capital with respect to technology development ($\alpha_{HA}$) is set at 0.45, capturing the learning-by-doing channel, as in \citet{ChangGomesSchorfheide2002}. The elasticity of public R\&D spending ($\alpha_{RD}$) is calibrated at 0.1, consistent with the R\&D impact estimates in \citet{FieldhouseMertens2023}.
    \item \emph{Technology adoption parameters.} Following \citet{CominGertler2006} and \citet{Anzoateguietal2019}, the elasticity of the probability of adoption with respect to adoption spending ($\varsigma$) is set at 0.8, and $\lambda_0$ is set at 0.1, implying an average lag of approximately ten years.
\end{compactitem}

In addition, the steady states of fiscal variables are set as follows.

\begin{compactitem}
    \item \emph{Public consumption-to-GDP ratio.} Set to standard values for each country group: 18 percent of GDP for AEs and 14 percent of GDP for EMDEs, based on \citet{IMF2023FM} and the World Bank's World Development Indicators database. Public consumption includes wages of teachers, doctors, and nurses, other public workers employed in education and health care, and other government final consumption.
    \item \emph{Public investment-to-GDP ratio.} Infrastructure investment is set at 3 percent of GDP for AEs and 5 percent of GDP for EMDEs (excluding public education and health investment, like building schools and hospitals). Public human capital spending (investing in public education and health) is calibrated at 1.5 percent of GDP for AEs and 2 percent of GDP for EMDEs, based on the share of these items in public investment of the OECD income group \citep{OECD2025Glance} due to limited data availability of public investment composition.
    \item \emph{Effective tax rates.} Effective tax rates are set at standard levels for each country group, following \citet{IMF2023FM}, \citet{Benitezetal2023}, and \citet{OECD2025Taxing}. The consumption tax rate is set at 18 percent for AEs and 15 percent for EMDEs. The income tax rate is 25 percent for AEs and 10 percent for EMDEs.
\end{compactitem}

%========================================================================
\section{Model properties} \label{sec:properties}
%========================================================================

This section documents the model's quantitative properties before turning to the policy reforms of Section~\ref{sec:results}.
It first reports the implied fiscal multipliers---explaining how each productive spending category transmits to output---and benchmarks them against the empirical literature.
It then examines how the responses depend on the model's key structural parameters.
Output responses are reported as percent deviations from the steady state.

\subsection{Fiscal multipliers}

The three productive spending instruments transmit to output over different horizons.
Infrastructure investment raises output relatively quickly, since public infrastructure capital feeds directly into the production function~\eqref{eq:production}.
Investment in human capital pays off more gradually, reflecting the slow accumulation of the human capital stock~\eqref{eq:humancapital}.
Public R\&D operates with the longest lag: it raises the stock of created technologies, which is adopted---and so reaches production---only over time (Eqs.~\ref{eq:Z}--\ref{eq:A}).
These differing horizons are summarized by the cumulative fiscal multipliers, which measure the discounted output gain per unit of additional spending.

The implied cumulative multipliers over 25 years for public investment in human capital and infrastructure are about 1 for AEs and 2.5 for EMDEs (Table~\ref{tab:multipliers}).
These are in the range of the long-run multipliers reported by \citet{Ramey2020}.
\citet{AfonsoRodrigues2024} also document that the multiplier of public investment is 0.9 for advanced economies and 2.3 for emerging economies.
The cumulative multiplier for R\&D expenditure is about 1.8, in line with \citet{FieldhouseMertens2023}.
The larger multipliers for EMDEs reflect the higher output elasticity of infrastructure and the higher marginal product of public capital in these economies.

\subsection{Sensitivity}

The output responses depend on the model's key structural parameters.
The most consequential is the speed of technology diffusion, governed by the adoption probability $\lambda_t$.
Figure~\ref{fig:diffusion} shows how the output gains from a 50/50 human-capital/R\&D spending mix depend on this speed: faster diffusion brings the productivity payoff of R\&D forward, raising gains at every horizon.
The responses are similarly sensitive to the output elasticity of infrastructure ($\alpha_G$), the elasticity of human capital formation with respect to the public stock ($\mu$), and the efficiency wedges $e^{GI}$, $e^{GE}$, and $e^{GRD}$, each of which scales the productive payoff of the corresponding spending category.

\section{Policy experiments} \label{sec:results}
%========================================================================

This section illustrates the model through a set of policy experiments.
Each experiment is a permanent, budget-neutral reform: an increase in a growth-enhancing spending category is financed by an equivalent cut in public consumption.
Output responses are reported as percent deviations from the steady state.
Unless stated otherwise, the experiments start in 2025 and the horizon extends to 2050.

\subsection{Reallocating expenditure toward investment}

Figure~\ref{fig:reallocation} shows output responses to a permanent increase of 1 percent of GDP in each expenditure category, funded by a cut in public consumption, for advanced economies (panel~a) and emerging market and developing economies (panel~b).
Reallocating toward infrastructure investment raises output relatively quickly, since infrastructure capital feeds directly into the production function (Eq.~\ref{eq:production}).
Reallocating toward human capital investment delivers gains that build up more slowly, reflecting the gradual accumulation of human capital (Eq.~\ref{eq:humancapital}).
The gains are larger for EMDEs, consistent with the higher output elasticity of infrastructure and the higher marginal product of public human capital in these economies.

\subsection{Combining human capital and R\&D}

In advanced economies, the innovation channel is active: public R\&D spending raises the stock of created technologies (Eq.~\ref{eq:Z}), which is then adopted with a lag (Eq.~\ref{eq:A}).
Figure~\ref{fig:humanCapital} shows output gains from increasing public investment in human capital, public R\&D spending, and a 50/50 mix of both, financed by cutting public consumption.

For EMDEs, reorienting resources toward human capital and infrastructure investment can deliver long-term gains, though over different time horizons.
A combination of the two is a viable option.
This policy mix results in a more balanced outcome by combining the short-term gains from infrastructure investment with the longer-term gains from human capital investment.
While this option delivers a slightly lower output gain by 2050 than investing more heavily in human capital alone, it yields higher output in the shorter term.

%========================================================================
\section{Conclusions} \label{sec:conclusions}
%========================================================================

This paper has documented a model that quantifies two distinct levers of public spending policy: the allocation of spending across categories and the efficiency with which spending is converted into productive capital.
By embedding expenditure-side fiscal policy and human capital formation in an endogenous growth framework, the model captures the different horizons over which infrastructure, human capital, and innovation pay off.
The simulations show that reallocating spending toward investment, and closing efficiency gaps, can raise output materially over the long run.
The gains are larger for emerging market and developing economies, which start further from the efficiency frontier and feature a higher marginal product of public capital.
The framework can be used to assess the long-run output consequences of country-specific spending reforms, and applications to individual economies are a natural next step.

\section{Equilibrium conditions} \label{app:equilibrium}
%========================================================================

This appendix collects the equilibrium conditions not stated in the main text. The system is solved in stationarized form: trending variables are scaled by $\Lambda_t$, and $g$ denotes the gross trend growth rate along the balanced-growth path. The complete model is implemented in the accompanying Dynare files.

\subsection{Technology adoption}

Competitive adopters value an adopted technology at $\mathcal{V}_t$ and an unadopted one at $\mathcal{J}_t$, discount with the household stochastic discount factor $\mathit{SDF}_{t+1}$, and choose adoption expenditure $S_t$. The optimality conditions are
\begin{align}
    \mathit{SDF}_{t+1} &= \beta\, \frac{C_t}{C_{t+1}}, \label{eq:sdf}\\
    \lambda_t &= \lambda_0\, \left(S_t\right)^{\varsigma}, \label{eq:probadopt}\\
    g\, A_{t+1} &= \lambda_t\, \phi\left(Z_t - A_t\right) + \phi A_t, \label{eq:Astat}\\
    \mathcal{V}_t &= \frac{\mu^{p}-1}{\mu^{p}}\, mc_t\, Y_t + \phi\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\mathit{SDF}_{t+1}\, \mathcal{V}_{t+1}\, \frac{A_{t}}{A_{t+1}}\, \frac{1}{g}\right], \label{eq:VA}\\
    \mathcal{J}_t &= -S_t + \phi\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\mathit{SDF}_{t+1}\, \frac{A_{t}}{A_{t+1}}\, \frac{1}{g}\left(\lambda_t\, \mathcal{V}_{t+1} + \left(1-\lambda_t\right)\mathcal{J}_{t+1}\right)\right], \label{eq:JZ}\\
    S_t &= \varsigma\, \lambda_t\, \phi\, \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[\mathit{SDF}_{t+1}\, \frac{1}{g}\, \frac{A_{t}}{A_{t+1}}\left(\mathcal{V}_{t+1} - \mathcal{J}_{t+1}\right)\right], \label{eq:SAfoc}\end{align}
where $\mu^{p}$ is the steady-state gross markup of intermediate-goods producers. Equation~\eqref{eq:Astat} is the stationarized counterpart of equation~\eqref{eq:A}.

\subsection{Fiscal rules}

The expenditure instruments follow
\begin{align}
    I_t^{GI} &= I^{GI} + Y^{d}\,\varepsilon_t^{gi}, \qquad
    I_t^{GE} = I^{GE} + Y^{d}\,\varepsilon_t^{ge}, \\
    G_t^{RD} - G^{RD} &= \rho_{RD}\left(G_{t-1}^{RD} - G^{RD}\right) + Y^{d}\,\varepsilon_t^{rd}, \\
    G_t^{C} - G^{C} &= -\left(I_t^{GI} - I^{GI} + I_t^{GE} - I^{GE} + G_t^{RD} - G^{RD}\right) + Y^{d}\,\varepsilon_t^{c},
\end{align}
where unstarred steady-state values carry no time index and $Y^{d}$ is steady-state demand. The last line implements the budget-neutral financing rule: any rise in investment or R\&D is offset one-for-one by government consumption, unless the consumption shock $\varepsilon_t^{c}$ is itself activated. Taxes and transfers stabilize debt,
\begin{align}
    \tau_t^{c} - \tau^{c} &= \rho_{\tau c}\left(\tau_{t-1}^{c} - \tau^{c}\right) + \left(1-\rho_{\tau c}\right)\gamma_{d}^{\tau c}\left(d_{t-1} - d^{*}\right) + \varepsilon_t^{\tau c}, \\
    \tau_t^{w} - \tau^{w} &= \rho_{\tau w}\left(\tau_{t-1}^{w} - \tau^{w}\right) + \left(1-\rho_{\tau w}\right)\gamma_{d}^{\tau w}\left(d_{t-1} - d^{*}\right) + \varepsilon_t^{\tau w}, \\
    T_t - T &= \rho_{T}\left(T_{t-1} - T\right) - \left(1-\rho_{T}\right)\gamma_{d}^{T}\left(d_{t-1} - d^{*}\right) Y^{d},
\end{align}
with $d_t = D_t / Y_t$ the debt-to-GDP ratio and $d^{*}$ its target.

\subsection{Monetary policy and sovereign spread}

The policy rate follows the Taylor rule~\eqref{eq:taylor}. The government borrowing rate and the debt-dependent spread are
\begin{align}
    \log R_t &= \rho_{RG} R_{t-1} + \left(1-\rho_{RG}\right)\left[\log R_t^{mp} + \Delta_t\left(d_{t-1} - d^{*}\right)\right] + \varepsilon_t^{spr}, \\
    \Delta_t &= p_t^{def}\, \Delta^{cost}, \qquad
    p_t^{def} = \frac{\exp\!\left(\eta_1 + \eta_2\, d_{t-1}\right)}{1 + \exp\!\left(\eta_1 + \eta_2\, d_{t-1}\right)},
\end{align}
where $p_t^{def}$ is the default probability and $\Delta^{cost}$ the associated unit loss.

\subsection{Market clearing and pricing}

Aggregate demand, the gross-output wedge, and the price-dispersion term satisfy
\begin{align}
    Y_t^{d} &= C_t + I_t + I_t^{GI} + G_t^{C} + I_t^{GE} + G_t^{RD} + S_t + \left(\frac{Z_{t-1}}{A_{t-1}} - 1\right) S_t, \\
    Y_t &= v_t^{p}\, Y_t^{d}, \qquad
    v_t^{p} = \theta_p\left(\frac{\Pi_{t-1}^{\chi_p}}{\Pi_t}\right)^{-\epsilon} v_{t-1}^{p} + \left(1-\theta_p\right)\left(\Pi_t^{*}\right)^{-\epsilon},
\end{align}
where the technology terms collect the resources $\mathcal{R}_t$ used in creation and adoption.
